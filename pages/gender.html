Section · Nature

Nature, power, and what counts as “natural”

Section note

This section brings together concepts that challenge the idea of nature as neutral, innocent, or self-explanatory. Anthropocentrism centers the human as measure of all value. Ecofeminism connects the domination of nature with the domination of women and other marginalized groups. Petroculture shows how fossil-fuel modernity shapes everyday life, imagination, and political power. Together, these ideas help us see that “nature” is never just out there. It is narrated, organized, exploited, and defended through culture, economics, gender, and ideology.

Core concept

Anthropocentrism

Anthropocentrism is the view that humans are the center of meaning, value, and moral concern. It treats the non-human world as background, property, or resource. In critical theory, this concept matters because it reveals how ideas of superiority and entitlement shape both environmental damage and social hierarchy.

Author card

Val Plumwood

Ecofeminist philosopher who critiqued human-centered reason and dualistic thinking.

The problem is not simply that humans use nature, but that dominant culture imagines humans as outside and above it. Paraphrased from Plumwood

Connected lens

Ecofeminism

Ecofeminism links environmental exploitation with patriarchy, colonialism, and other systems of domination. It argues that the same logic that treats nature as disposable often treats women, Indigenous people, queer people, and racialized communities as expendable too.

Author card

What is presented as development often depends on the destruction of lives, lands, and knowledges. Inspired by ecofeminist critique

System

Petroculture

Petroculture names the way fossil fuels shape habits, infrastructure, aesthetics, fantasies of freedom, and political life. Oil is not only an energy source. It becomes a way of organizing movement, comfort, consumption, and national power.

Author card

Cultural theorist known for work on energy humanities and petroculture.

Why it matters

Nature is political, not neutral

These concepts belong together because they expose a pattern: what gets called natural is often what power wants to make unquestionable. Human supremacy, extractive economics, and gendered domination are not separate stories. They overlap, reinforce one another, and shape both policy and everyday life. Reading them together helps us move from isolated facts to structural critique.

“Natural” is often the name given to a social order that has learned how to disguise itself. Queerology note
