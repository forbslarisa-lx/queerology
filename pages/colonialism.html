Colonialism

A curated wall of concepts exploring how colonial power orders people, land, language, knowledge, and identity long after formal empire ends.

Core concept

Colonialism

Colonialism is a system in which one power occupies, rules, extracts from, and reorganizes another land and its people. It is not only military control or territorial conquest. It also reshapes language, law, education, religion, labor, and ideas of who is fully human. Colonialism produces hierarchies that continue to shape the present.

Frantz Fanon
Psychiatrist, anti-colonial thinker, and critic of empire
Colonial domination does not stop at land. It enters the mind, the body, and the social order.

Mechanism

Othering

Othering is the process by which people are marked as different, inferior, dangerous, backward, or outside the norm. Colonial systems depend on this logic. They construct the colonized as an “other” in order to justify control, civilizing missions, surveillance, and exclusion. Othering turns difference into hierarchy.

Edward Said
Literary critic and theorist of Orientalism
The “other” is often invented not to describe difference, but to stabilize power.

Response

Decolonization

Decolonization is not just the transfer of state power or the lowering of a colonial flag. It is the dismantling of colonial structures, habits, and ways of knowing. It asks what it would mean to return land, restore dignity, challenge imposed categories, and undo the idea that Europe is the measure of history, knowledge, and humanity.

Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o
Writer and critic of colonial language and culture
To decolonize is also to recover the right to name the world in one’s own terms.

Why it matters

Coloniality after empire

Formal empire may end, but colonial logics often remain. They survive in borders, development discourse, racial hierarchy, extraction, academic canon, migration regimes, and assumptions about modernity. This is why colonialism cannot be treated as only a past event. It is also a structure that continues to organize the present.

María Lugones
Philosopher of coloniality, gender, and power
Colonial rule does not disappear when administrations leave. It can persist as a way the world is sorted.
