Queerology · Power

Power

A curated wall of concepts exploring how power regulates bodies, defines norms, classifies lives, produces knowledge, and decides who is protected, corrected, exposed, or abandoned.

Mechanism

Institutions

Power does not float above society as an abstract force. It lives in institutions: the family, school, clinic, prison, church, border regime, military, media, and state. Institutions make power durable by turning norms into routines. They decide what counts as healthy, legal, respectable, intelligent, deviant, risky, or worthy of protection.

Michel Foucault
Theorist of institutions, discipline, and modern power
Power becomes ordinary when it is built into the places that organize daily life.

Norms

Normativity

Normativity is the quiet architecture of power. It defines what feels natural, proper, healthy, civilized, feminine, masculine, productive, or moral. Once a norm is established, difference can be framed as failure, pathology, danger, or defect. This is how power hides: not only by punishing deviation, but by making the norm feel like common sense.

Sara Ahmed
Scholar of norms, orientation, and institutional power
A norm is most effective when it disappears into the background and starts to feel like reality itself.
Reading shelf
  • Queer Phenomenology
  • Living a Feminist Life
Core concept

Biopolitics

Biopolitics describes how modern power manages life. It tracks populations, regulates reproduction, health, sexuality, hygiene, productivity, and risk. Instead of ruling only through spectacular punishment, modern states increasingly govern by optimizing life: measuring it, improving it, correcting it, and sorting it into normal and abnormal forms.

Michel Foucault
Philosopher of biopower, discipline, and state rationality
Modern power does not only threaten death. It administers life.

Afterlife of power

Necropolitics

Necropolitics asks a darker question: if biopolitics manages life, who gets pushed toward death, disposability, exposure, and abandonment? Some groups are not protected by the state but rendered killable, neglectable, or permanently vulnerable. Power does not only produce life chances. It also decides whose suffering is acceptable, invisible, or politically useful.

Achille Mbembe
Political theorist of sovereignty, violence, and death-worlds
Sovereignty is revealed most clearly in the power to decide who may live and who may be left to die.

Medical power

Medicalization

Medicalization is the process by which social, moral, sexual, or behavioral differences are redefined as medical issues. It gives institutions the authority to diagnose, classify, correct, and normalize. Queer lives, disabled bodies, reproductive choices, and nonconforming genders have often been governed through this lens. Medical language can sound neutral, even caring, while quietly converting difference into disorder and political control into treatment.

Peter Conrad
Sociologist of medicalization and diagnosis
To medicalize is often to move a question from ethics or politics into the authority of diagnosis.
Reading shelf

Violent optimization

Eugenics

Eugenics is the fantasy of improving the population by controlling who may reproduce, belong, or be considered fully valuable. It combines science, nationalism, ableism, racism, class hierarchy, and reproductive control. Eugenics is not just a historical horror. Its logic survives in sterilization, border sorting, genetic panic, fitness narratives, and policies that rank some lives as more desirable than others.

Angela Davis
Scholar of prisons, race, and reproductive justice
The dream of “better populations” is never neutral. It is built from hierarchies of whose future matters.

Why it matters

Surveillance, Law, and Resistance

Power is not only top-down force. It is also the network of files, forms, data, police, diagnoses, documentation, school rules, border checks, algorithms, and public narratives that decide who becomes legible and who becomes suspect. But power is never total. People resist through refusal, community, mutual care, reclaiming language, breaking categories, exposing institutions, and building other ways to live. Power draws the map. Resistance redraws it.

Judith Butler
Philosopher of precarity, recognizability, and political resistance
Where life is constrained by power, politics begins with the struggle over whose lives become livable.
