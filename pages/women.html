Women

A layered exploration of how “woman” is not a fixed identity but a historical, political, and contested category shaped through power, exclusion, resistance, and continuous redefinition.

Historical rupture

The Birth of “Woman” as a Category

The category “woman” has not always existed in its current form. Modern gender categories emerged alongside Enlightenment thinking, colonial expansion, and the structuring of rights. Who counted as a “woman” was always political — often excluding enslaved women, colonized women, and working-class women.

Olympe de Gouges
Author of the Declaration of the Rights of Woman
Woman is born free and lives equal to man in her rights.
  • Declaration of the Rights of Woman
Core rupture

“One is not born, but becomes a woman”

Womanhood is not biological destiny but a social process. It is produced through norms, expectations, education, and power structures. Becoming a woman means being shaped by a system that defines what femininity should be.

Simone de Beauvoir
Existentialist philosopher
One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman.
  • The Second Sex
System

Patriarchy

Patriarchy is a system where power is structured through male dominance. It shapes institutions, knowledge, labor, and expectations. It is not just about men vs women — it is a system that organizes hierarchy and control.

bell hooks
Feminist theorist
Patriarchy has no gender. It is a system we are all socialized into.
  • Feminism is for Everybody
Lens

Intersectionality

Women are not a single group. Race, class, sexuality, and ability shape how womanhood is lived. Intersectionality shows how systems overlap, producing very different realities for different women.

Kimberlé Crenshaw
Legal scholar
If you see only gender, you miss how power actually works.

Core concept

Gender as Structure

Gender organizes society: labor, care, reproduction, value, and visibility. Women’s work has historically been undervalued, invisible, or unpaid. Gender is not just identity — it is infrastructure.

Silvia Federici
Feminist Marxist theorist
Reproductive labor is the hidden foundation of capitalism.

Women’s bodies have been regulated through medicine, law, and morality. From reproductive rights to beauty standards, control over bodies is central to maintaining power structures.

Structure

The Canon and the Making of “Woman”

The literary and academic canon defines which stories are preserved, taught, and treated as universal. It shapes what counts as knowledge, culture, and “human experience.” Historically, this canon has been overwhelmingly shaped by male authors, male perspectives, and male protagonists.

As a result, women have often appeared not as full subjects, but as limited narrative roles: the mother, the muse, the seductress, the witch, the victim, or the moral warning. Even when central to the plot, they are frequently defined in relation to male characters — as love interests, obstacles, or symbols, rather than autonomous agents.

These patterns do not simply reflect reality. They actively shape it. Repeated representations produce expectations about what women are, what they should desire, how they should behave, and what kinds of lives are imaginable or acceptable for them.

Feminist and queer critique has shown that the canon is not neutral. It is a site of power: it includes, excludes, elevates, and silences. What is left out is often treated as marginal, personal, or less serious — even when it reflects the experiences of the majority.

Expanding the canon is not only about adding more voices. It is about questioning the idea of universality itself: asking whose stories have been treated as “human,” and whose have been reduced to category, stereotype, or exception.

Laura Mulvey · bell hooks
Feminist film theory · cultural criticism
The power to tell stories is also the power to define who counts as fully human.

Why it matters

Reclaiming “Woman”

The category “woman” is not static. It is continuously redefined through feminist, queer, and trans struggles. It can exclude, but it can also be reclaimed, expanded, and transformed.

Judith Butler
Philosopher of gender and performativity
Categories are never stable. That is where change becomes possible.
