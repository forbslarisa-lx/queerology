Queerology · Race

Race

A curated wall of concepts exploring how race is invented, institutionalized, normalized, and lived through classification, power, history, and social reality.

Dominant framework

Classical race theory · U.S. perspective

This framework emphasizes race as a structured hierarchy built through white supremacy, scientific classification, exclusion, and systemic inequality. It helps explain how race became institutionalized and reproduced through law, education, media, science, and everyday life.

Mechanism

Othering

Othering is the process through which a group is marked as different, inferior, dangerous, or outside the norm. In racial systems, othering turns difference into hierarchy and makes exclusion feel natural, justified, and ordinary.

Edward Said
Literary critic and theorist of representation
The “other” is often produced not to describe difference, but to stabilize power.

Norm

Whiteness

Whiteness functions not only as an identity category but as an invisible norm, shaping who is seen as universal, neutral, rational, and fully belonging. It often appears most powerful when it is unnamed.

Richard Dyer
Scholar of whiteness and visual culture
Power is often hardest to see when it presents itself as ordinary.

History

Scientific Racism

Scientific racism refers to the use of supposedly objective methods to rank human groups and justify inequality. Craniometry, taxonomy, eugenics, and racial classification transformed political domination into something that looked measurable, neutral, and rational.

Stephen Jay Gould
Historian of science and critic of racial measurement
Numbers can look natural even when they are built to confirm hierarchy.
Reading shelf
  • The Mismeasure of Man
  • Race, science, and classification
Core concept

Race as a Social Construct

Race is not a biological truth. It is a social and political system for sorting people, attaching meaning to bodily difference, and organizing hierarchy. This does not make race unreal. It makes it historically produced, institutionally reinforced, and lived in everyday life.

Michael Omi & Howard Winant
Theorists of racial formation
Race is made, remade, and defended through social struggle, not discovered as a natural fact.

  • Racial Formation in the United States
  • Race and social construction
Why it matters

Race, Power, and Modernity

Race persists not because biology proves it, but because modern societies continue to reproduce it through policing, migration regimes, media, education, housing, wealth, and memory. Even when race is declared socially invented, its material effects remain structured into everyday life.

Paul Gilroy
Scholar of race, culture, and postcolonial modernity
A category can be historically invented and still shape the world with brutal consistency.

  • Race and modernity

Race is not structured in a single way across contexts. Different histories produce different logics of classification, belonging, exclusion, and perceived proximity to the norm.

Alternative framework

Layered race formations · Europe and post-Soviet contexts

In many contexts, racism does not operate through a single black–white binary. Instead, groups are positioned through overlapping hierarchies of foreignness, cultural proximity, migration, language, class, religion, and conditional access to belonging. Here, race often works through shifting gradations rather than a single visible divide.

Process

Racialization

Racialization is the process through which groups come to be treated as racialized, even when difference is framed as cultural, linguistic, national, or civilizational. Race is not always named directly. It is often built through repeated associations with danger, backwardness, foreignness, or excess.

Stuart Hall
Theorist of race, ideology, and representation
Difference becomes racial when power repeatedly teaches society how to read it.

Structure

Racial Triangulation

Racial triangulation shows that groups are not positioned along a single line of superiority and inferiority. They may be relatively valorized in one respect and excluded in another, praised as useful or disciplined while still marked as foreign, suspect, or not fully belonging. This prevents solidarity and stabilizes dominant power.

Claire Jean Kim
Political theorist of racial triangulation
Groups can be positioned as both valued and excluded, closer to the norm yet never inside it.

Hierarchy

Colorism

Colorism names hierarchies based on skin tone within and across racialized groups. It often reflects colonial histories, beauty standards, and proximity to whiteness, showing that racial power works not only between groups, but also within them.

Alice Walker
Writer who popularized the term colorism
Hierarchy can survive even inside communities shaped by the same system.

Belonging

Conditional Whiteness / Belonging

Some groups are not fully excluded, but never fully included. Their belonging remains conditional: dependent on language, behavior, class performance, usefulness, and conformity to dominant norms. Acceptance can always be withdrawn.

Sara Ahmed
Scholar of orientation, belonging, and institutional fit
To be allowed near the center is not the same as being treated as part of it.

Historical logic

Coloniality

Coloniality names the persistence of colonial logics after formal empire ends. Hierarchies of civilization, backwardness, modernity, and human value continue to shape how populations are sorted. This is crucial for understanding Europe, Russia, internal empire, borderlands, and the racialization of the “South.”

María Lugones
Philosopher of coloniality, gender, and power
Empire can leave the map and remain in the social imagination.

Discipline

Respectability Politics

Respectability politics describes the pressure on marginalized groups to become acceptable by performing discipline, morality, respectability, or assimilation. It promises conditional recognition while leaving the larger hierarchy untouched.

Evelyn Brooks Higginbotham
Historian of respectability politics
Respectability can become a survival script inside an unjust order.

Gradient

Intra-white Hierarchy

Whiteness is not always experienced as a fixed border. In many contexts it functions as a gradient, producing distinctions between “real Europeans,” “eastern,” “southern,” “borderland,” or “not-quite-white” populations. This helps explain forms of racism that unfold inside nominal whiteness.

Alastair Bonnett
Scholar of whiteness, Europe, and racial boundaries
Even the norm can fracture into inner circles and outer edges.

Position

Migrantization

Migrantization is the process through which people are treated as migrants regardless of their legal status, biography, or actual belonging. Accent, name, clothing, religion, neighborhood, or perceived origin can mark someone as permanently outside the national body. Here, “migrant” becomes less a fact than a racialized social position.

Paul Mecheril
Scholar of migration, belonging, and racialized difference
One can live inside a nation and still be read as arriving from outside it.
