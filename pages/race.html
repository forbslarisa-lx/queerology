Race

A curated wall of concepts exploring how race is invented, institutionalized, normalized, and lived through classification, power, history, and social reality.

Mechanism

Othering

Othering is the process through which a group is marked as different, inferior, dangerous, or outside the norm. In racial systems, othering turns difference into hierarchy. It makes exclusion feel natural and helps justify segregation, surveillance, fear, and unequal belonging.

Edward Said
Literary critic and theorist of representation
The “other” is often produced not to describe difference, but to stabilize power.

Norm

Whiteness

Whiteness is not just an identity category. It also functions as an invisible norm against which others are measured. It shapes who is seen as universal, neutral, rational, and fully belonging, often without naming itself as a position of power.

Richard Dyer
Scholar of whiteness and visual culture
Power is often hardest to see when it presents itself as ordinary.

Core concept

Race as a Social Construct

Race is not a fixed biological truth. It is a social and political system for sorting people, attaching meaning to bodily difference, and organizing hierarchy. This does not make race unreal. It makes it historically produced. Race becomes real through law, science, institutions, violence, representation, and everyday life.

Michael Omi & Howard Winant
Theorists of racial formation
Race is made, remade, and enforced through social struggle, not discovered as a neutral fact of nature.

History

Scientific Racism

Scientific racism used the language of objectivity, biology, and measurement to legitimize racial hierarchy. Classification, skull measurement, eugenics, and pseudo-scientific ranking turned prejudice into “evidence” and helped institutions present domination as reason.

Stephen Jay Gould
Historian of science and critic of racial measurement
Numbers can look neutral even when they are built to confirm hierarchy.

Why it matters

Race, Power, and Modernity

Race persists not because biology proves it, but because modern societies continue to reproduce it through policing, migration regimes, media, education, wealth, housing, and memory. Even when race is declared socially invented, its material effects remain structured into everyday life. That is why race must be studied as both fiction and force.

Paul Gilroy
Scholar of race, culture, and postcolonial modernity
A category can be historically invented and still shape the world with brutal consistency.
