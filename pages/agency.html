Queerology · Agency

Agency

Agency is not the fantasy of total freedom. It is the question of how action becomes possible inside norms, structures, pressure, desire, and unequal conditions of life.

Classical framework

Action inside structure

Classical theories of agency ask whether people truly act, or whether action is shaped in advance by institutions, norms, class position, socialization, and power. This framework does not deny action, but it resists the myth of the fully autonomous self. Agency appears here as constrained, patterned, strategic, and unevenly distributed.

Core problem

Structure vs Agency

Agency names the capacity to act, choose, improvise, and influence the world. But critical theory immediately complicates this idea: action never takes place in neutral conditions. We act through institutions, categories, classed and gendered expectations, embodied histories, and unequal access to power.

The classical problem of social theory is therefore not whether people act, but how action is possible inside structures that precede them. Agency is real, but never unlimited. It operates through a field of constraints, incentives, habits, risks, and recognitions that shape what even feels thinkable.

Anthony Giddens
Structuration theory, action, and social reproduction
We act, but not in conditions we choose.

Contemporary theory complicates agency even further. It asks whether action must always look like resistance, how desire and affect shape choice, and how neoliberal, digital, and precarious conditions reorganize what agency even means.

Contemporary rethinking

Agency after autonomy

Contemporary theory challenges the liberal fantasy that agency means independent, rational self-direction. It asks what happens when subjects are formed through subordination, when optimism attaches us to impossible futures, when feeling moves us as much as reason, and when algorithms, precarity, and time itself begin to govern action from within.

Subordination

Affective force

Affect and Orientation

Action is not driven by reason alone. Fear, comfort, shame, desire, attachment, and aversion orient bodies toward some possibilities and away from others. Agency can therefore be affective before it is deliberate.

What feels close, familiar, safe, or imaginable matters politically. People do not simply choose from a neutral menu of options. They are pulled, repelled, exhausted, encouraged, and shaped through affective worlds that organize movement in advance.

Sara Ahmed

We move not only through choice, but through feeling.
