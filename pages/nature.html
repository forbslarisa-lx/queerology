Nature

A curated wall of concepts exploring how “nature” is imagined, governed, extracted, gendered, and defended through ideology, economics, and culture.

Core concept

Anthropocentrism

Anthropocentrism places the human at the center of value, meaning, and moral concern. It treats the non-human world as background, property, or resource. This concept helps explain why environmental destruction is so often normalized: nature is framed as something to manage, consume, or master rather than something humans are inseparable from.

Val Plumwood
Ecofeminist philosopher and critic of human-centered reason
The problem is not simply that humans use nature, but that dominant culture imagines humans as outside and above it.

Lens

Ecofeminism

Ecofeminism links the domination of nature to the domination of women and other marginalized groups. It argues that exploitation often follows the same logic: what is feminized, colonized, racialized, or naturalized becomes easier to control, extract from, and dismiss.

Vandana Shiva
Scholar and activist linking ecology, colonialism, and gendered power
What is presented as development often depends on the destruction of lands, lives, and knowledges.

System

Petroculture

Petroculture names the way fossil fuels shape not just industry, but also movement, comfort, imagination, aesthetics, and political life. Oil is not only an energy source. It becomes a cultural logic that organizes how modern life feels ordinary, mobile, and desirable.

Imre Szeman
Cultural theorist known for work on energy humanities and petroculture
Oil is not only an energy source. It is a way of organizing modern life.

Why it matters

Nature as a Political Category

These concepts belong together because they show that “nature” is never just a neutral background. It is narrated, owned, defended, and exploited through power. What gets called natural is often what dominant systems want to make unquestionable. Reading nature politically reveals how ecology, gender, class, empire, and extraction intersect.

Donna Haraway
Scholar of science, nature, and situated knowledge
Nature is not a place of innocence. It is a field of stories, struggle, relation, and power.
