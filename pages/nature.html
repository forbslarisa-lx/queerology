Nature

A curated wall of concepts exploring how “nature” is imagined, governed, exploited, and defended through power, extraction, gender, and culture.

Core concept

Anthropocentrism

Anthropocentrism places the human at the center of value, meaning, and moral concern. It treats the non-human world as background, property, or raw material. This concept helps explain why environmental destruction is so often normalized: nature is framed as something to manage, consume, or master rather than something humans are inseparable from.

Val Plumwood

Ecofeminist philosopher and critic of human-centered reason

“The problem is not simply that humans use nature, but that dominant culture imagines humans as outside and above it.”

Connected lens

Ecofeminism

Ecofeminism connects the exploitation of nature with the exploitation of women and other marginalized groups. It shows how domination often works through the same logic: what is feminized, racialized, colonized, or naturalized becomes easier to control, extract from, or dismiss.

Vandana Shiva

Scholar and activist linking ecology, colonialism, and gendered power

“What is presented as development often depends on the destruction of lands, lives, and knowledges.”

System

Petroculture

Petroculture names the way fossil fuels shape not just industry, but imagination, movement, comfort, aesthetics, and everyday life. Oil becomes culture. It organizes infrastructure, desire, and political power so deeply that extractive systems begin to feel ordinary, inevitable, even invisible.

Imre Szeman

Cultural theorist known for work on energy humanities and petroculture

“Oil is not only an energy source. It is a way of organizing modern life.”

Why it matters

Nature is political, not neutral

These ideas belong together because they reveal a shared pattern: what gets called natural is often what power wants to make unquestionable. Human supremacy, extractive economics, gendered domination, and environmental collapse are not separate stories. They overlap, reinforce one another, and shape both policy and everyday life.

Donna Haraway

Scholar of science, nature, and situated knowledge

“Nature is not a place of innocence. It is a field of stories, power, struggle, and relation.”
